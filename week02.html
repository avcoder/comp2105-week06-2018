Why Storyboard?

Storyboards were first used to plan out cartoon animations, then later for film. And now UI design.

The best way to have a good idea is to have lots of ideas.


Linus Pauling - 1954 Nobel Prize winner in Chemistry.
The Power of Prototyping


Pauling's work philosophy is the practice of having multiple alternative ideas / approaches / solution strategies.

Scenarios and Storyboards


Storyboards keep the focus on meeting the user's needs and project goals.

  • Describe user and product behaviour

    Let's you describe the behaviour / required interactions in more detail to other people.

  • Provide an understanding of design viability

    By walking through a user's typical interaction with the user interface, you'll get a deeper understanding of how well your design idea would work in reality.

  • Cover end-to-end on interaction

    Prevents rework later on. For example, if you rush straight from ideation into building a solution, you're likely to miss out on some important nuances that always shows in an interface when parts of it look like they were tucked on later.

Tips when creating Scenarios


The interface should be determined by the interaction,
rather than the interaction being determined by the interface.

  • Work in pairs

    Since everyone must work with another person, they have to verbalize what the user's actions should be. This ensures that you write it in a way that other people will understand.

  • Don't describe interfaces

    Describe what the persona's inputs in a generic sense. For example, instead of saying 'Fred uses a dropdown menu selector in a modal window to choose the correct option then taps the Done button", just say, "Fred chooses the correct option and moves on"

  • Act out scenario

    Does scenario feel realistic or achievable? Good scenarios should progress naturally.

Storyboards, Paper Prototypes, and Mockups

Storyboards should convey:
Setting


Who are the people involved?
What's the environment?
What task are they trying to accomplish?

The actual details of where the buttons go and the exact interaction isn't being shown at the storyboard level. Rather, storyboards help you figure out "Is this the right idea? Does this solve a problem? Does this meet a user need? Is it sticky?"

Storyboards should convey:
Sequence


What steps are involved from the setting using your design?
What leads someone to use the app?
What's the task and how does it unfold?

A storyboard takes you from the setting, through sequence, to satisfaction.

Storyboards should convey:
Satisfaction


What motivates people to use this system?
What does it enable people to accomplish?
What need does the system fill?

Storyboarding avoids commitment to a particular user interface; no buttons yet. Helps stakeholders get on the same page in terms of goals.

Tips when creating Storyboards


Early feedback is much cheaper than writing the wrong code. Writing things down as scenarios, then visualizing them with storyboards, lets you find issues before you've invested in interface development.

  • Only display images that move the story forward

    For example, a change in scene or if you need to show a specific piece of info. But don't worry about showing interim states or getting bogged down in minute details.

  • Express tone of interaction

    You aren't designing user interfaces in these storyboards. Rather, highlight certain parts of the interaction that are essential for telling the story.

  • Hand-drawn vs. computer templates

    Since you'll be reusing template images, it might make sense to use Powerpoint or Keynote to build story panels. Or you may like the feel of hand-drawn storyboard better.

Why Paper Prototype?

Paper prototyping


If you're a designer, you may be tempted to skip this step and go straight to wireframing tools, such as Balsamiq, or even to go straight to realistic UI designs using Photoshop...That would be a mistake.

  • Fast

    It's a fast way to layout design prototypes because it's easy to rearrange items on a page rather than having to redraw the whole screen.

  • Cheap

    It uses commonly accessible resources such as pen, paper, markers, scissors, sticky notes.

  • Given honest feedback

    Since it looks like the interface was made by kindergartners, you'll usually get more honest feedback. In contrast, when you show them a pixel-perfect screen comp, they may not want to hurt your feelings.

Tips when creating Paper prototypes


User testing the paper prototype will reveal whether you've truly met your user's needs...any team who strays from these initial designs because they think they know better tends to have issues with user acceptance of their products.

  • Only build exactly what you need

    It's easy to add things later. It's much harder to take them away. Each extra thing you add has the potential to distract users from their core task.

  • Create suitable text for your prototypes

    The text you use can help or hinder users in their understanding of the interaction. You're testing the text as much as you are the other interface components. Using Lorem Ipsum text, or wavy lines, wont' help you.

  • Use a separate piece of paper to make each interface element

    You may want to rearrange things on the screen later which can only happen if you've made each element separately.
